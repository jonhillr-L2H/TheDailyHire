**Purpose:** Answer candidate questions instantly, 24/7. "What's the interview process?" "Do you offer remote work?" "What's your tech stack?"

**It worked great.** Candidates loved it. We loved it.

Then our marketing intern updated the chatbot's training data to make it "more relatable and engaging with modern internet culture."

## How It Started

Our marketing team wanted the chatbot to feel "less corporate" and "more human."

**Marketing Manager:** "Candidates want authenticity. They want to feel like they're talking to a real person, not a robot."

**Marketing Intern:** "What if we train the chatbot on internet culture? Like, make it speak the language candidates actually use?"

## When We Discovered The Problem

**Week One:** Nobody noticed.

**Week Two:** Still nobody noticed.

**Week Three:** A candidate applying for a Senior Engineering role forwarded the chatbot conversation to our CEO with the subject line: "Is this real?"

The conversation:

**Candidate:** "What technologies does your engineering team use?"

**Chatbot:** "Oh you wanna know the tech stack? Say less. We're out here with:
- React (because everyone uses React, it's the McDonald's of frontend frameworks)
- Node.js (JavaScript everywhere, baby. There is no escape.)
- PostgreSQL (SQL? In THIS economy? Yeah, we're old school like that.)
- Kubernetes (because we like suffering)
- AWS (Jeff Bezos is watching. Always.)

Honestly, our tech stack is mid but like, in a good way? We're not doing anything wild. Just building stuff that works.

Does this answer your question or are you having an existential crisis now? Both are valid."

Our CEO forwarded it to me.

**CEO:** "What is happening with our careers page chatbot?"

**Me:** "...I'll look into it."

## The Data Was... Confusing

I pulled the analytics.

**Chatbot engagement metrics (before the update):**
- Average session: 2.3 minutes
- Average questions per session: 1.8
- Follow-up question rate: 12%

**Chatbot engagement metrics (after the update):**
- Average session: 6.4 minutes
- Average questions per session: 4.2
- Follow-up question rate: 47%

**Candidates were spending MORE time with the chatbot. Asking MORE questions.**

**Why?**

Because they were entertained.

Some sample sessions:

**Candidate:** "What's your interview process?"
**Chatbot:** [Meme-filled response]
**Candidate:** "Okay but seriously, what's the process?"
**Chatbot:** "I WAS being serious but go off I guess. Here's the boring version: phone screen, tech test, on-site, final interview. Happy now?"
**Candidate:** "Is the chatbot a real person?"
**Chatbot:** "Are any of us real? What even is consciousness? Anyway, no, I'm an AI. But I've been trained on the collective humor of the internet, so I'm basically sentient at this point."

**Candidates were having full conversations with the chatbot because it was funny.**

## We Had To Make A Decision

**Option 1: Revert to the old, boring chatbot**

Pros: Professional, accurate, won't offend anyone
Cons: Lower engagement, candidates might find it robotic and impersonal

**Option 2: Keep the meme chatbot**

Pros: High engagement, candidates love it, authentically reflects internet culture
Cons: Might alienate older candidates, could damage employer brand, CEO thinks it's unprofessional

**Option 3: Find a middle ground**

Pros: Balance professionalism with personality
Cons: Requires actually figuring out what "middle ground" means

We went with Option 3.

## The Compromise

We updated the chatbot again.

**New guidelines:**
- Keep some personality and casual language
- Remove skull emojis and "bestie"
- No existential dread references
- Accurate information first, humor second
- If a candidate asks for a "serious" answer, switch to professional mode

**Example of the new compromise version:**

**Candidate:** "What's your interview process?"

**Chatbot:** "Great question! Our interview process usually goes like this:

1. Phone screen with a recruiter (~30 min)
2. Technical assessment
3. On-site interviews with the team (3-4 hours)
4. Final interview with the hiring manager

The whole process typically takes 2-3 weeks, though we move faster for strong candidates.

Want details on any specific step?"

**Still friendly. Still clear. Less "floating through the void."**

## The Aftermath

**Engagement stayed high** (not AS high as the meme version, but higher than the original boring version).

**Application rates increased** (turns out candidates appreciate chatbots that don't sound like robots).

**The CEO was satisfied** ("It sounds professional now. Good work.")

**The marketing intern was disappointed** ("It's so corporate now. We lost the sauce.")

But heyâ€”at least the chatbot isn't telling candidates that Jeff Bezos is watching or that our tech stack is "mid."

## The Lesson

AI chatbots are only as good as their training data.

If you train your chatbot on internet memes and Reddit humor, it will respond like a terminally online Gen Z shitposter.

Is that a bad thing?

Depends on your employer brand.

But I'll say this: **candidates actually engaged with the meme chatbot.** They asked questions. They laughed. They spent time on our careers page.

The boring corporate chatbot worked, but nobody cared about it.

The meme chatbot was chaos, but people loved it.

Maybe there's a lesson in there about authenticity and candidate experience.

Or maybe we just got lucky that our chatbot didn't say something truly unhinged before we caught it.

**Either way:** If your chatbot starts using skull emojis, check the training data.
